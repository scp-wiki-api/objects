**Item №:** SCP-9334

**Object Class:** Contained

**Storage Details:** Permanently stored at Warehouse-8 in Site-19. As a precaution, their container is buried underneath 7 kg of salt. No testing applications have been identified, and inspection or handling is discouraged. There are no faces in the masks.

**Description:** Discovered by PVT J. Knowles on Kolombangara Island, 1944. Item SCP-9334 are a set of three gas masks speculated to have been manufactured in Japan around 1932-1934. Knowles wrote in his report that the masks had been found near the village of Poporo with their rims caked in what appeared to have been dried blood.

Knowles was allowed to carry SCP-9334 back with him to his family home in Jefferson, Arkansas following his discharge in 1946. SCP-9334 remained there for five years, during which time Knowles married and had two children. 

Neighbors of Knowles and family would first report strange odors on the 19th of September, 1954. This was originally thought to be the result of a broken gas pipe, as Jefferson had seen disrepair in the years after the war. Mrs. Knowles was among fourteen volunteers who searched for this pipe, however none was found. After Mr. Knowles' death in 1959 to lung cancer, SCP-9334 were donated to a local pawn shop. They would resurface in records in 1962 in the Brooklyn apartment of advertising firm associate G. Bardin. It is around this time that reports of unusual, far-away "blasts" heard in Jefferson ceased entirely. 

Bardin reported foul odors to his landlord that year, and in 1963 reported the same to the police. These odors made Bardin feel ill, and in some cases caused vomiting and migraines. On April 4th, 1963, Bardin admitted himself to the Wyckoff Heights Medical Center after apparently injuring his face during sleep; medical records show that Bardin had habitually scratched the edges of his jaw, temples, and upper forehead while asleep for a period possibly exceeding 40 days. None of the cuts were sufficiently deep enough for stitches, and Bardin was administered Betadine and pHisoHex after an overnight stay at the hospital. 

Following his discharge, Bardin continued to report intermittent foul odors within his apartment. A gas utility inspection conducted in May of 1963 found no irregularities in the building or the surrounding block.

Bardin was admitted to Wyckoff Heights a second time in August of 1963, following an altercation with neighbor H. Gayman. Records from this admittance are incomplete and partially illegible. Available documentation indicates that Bardin, while attempting to locate the source of an odor he believed originated from the adjacent unit, struck Mrs. Gayman repeatedly about the face and neck. Bardin was manic during questioning, saying that he was apparently attempting to remove something from Gayman's face. Notably, there are no faces in the masks.

After Bardin's institutionalization later that year, his personal effects were collected by his employer and direct supervisor, F. Dean. Dean retained SCP-9334 and displayed them on a shelf in his private office at the firm, behind his desk and facing the door.

Within several weeks, employees sharing Dean's floor began reporting persistent odors consistent with those described by every previous owner of SCP-9334. Complaints of headaches and nausea became frequent enough that building management commissioned a private air quality survey in early 1964. The survey returned entirely normal results, and no source was ever located.

During this period an administrative assistant, M. Vallelonga, developed a severe skin rash across her forehead. The affected area formed a narrow band stretching from one temple to the other, following a line that did not correspond to any garment or accessory she wore. There are no faces in the masks.

Over the following months, at least nine employees presented with thin, evenly spaced lacerations tracing the full perimeter of the face. In each case the cuts ran along the hairline, descended in front of the ears, and met beneath the jaw. There are no faces in the masks. Affected individuals reported no memory of injuring themselves and no awareness of the wounds until others pointed them out. The lacerations were remarkably uniform in depth and spacing across every person who exhibited them, a consistency that medical staff found difficult to reconcile with self-inflicted injury. There are no faces in the masks.

In July of 1964, Dean was arrested after leaving his home during the night at a full sprint and assaulting a pedestrian several blocks from his home. Dean stated that he had heard sustained gunfire and had been trying to reach cover.

Throughout the latter half of 1964, Dean's colleagues noted a steady deterioration in both his behavior and his appearance. He grew withdrawn, kept the blinds of his office permanently drawn, and was observed on numerous occasions pressing and pulling at the sides of his own face while alone at his desk. There are no faces in the masks. Mrs. Dean reported several mental outbursts by Mr. Dean upon her gazing upon his face. He would attempt to hide his face, and had repeated bouts of vomiting due to the odors that became so frequent that Mrs. Dean tired of cleaning them, choosing instead to stay at her sister's apartment. 

On February 24th, Mr. Dean was found dead behind his home with his face [There are no faces in the masks.]. There are no faces in the masks.

An analyst working for the government would soon flag an unusual cluster of unexplained medical reports filed within a single postal area. There are no faces in the masks. After consultation, Mrs. Dean donated SCP-9334 to the government on March 3rd, 1965. They were stored temporarily at [33 Thomas Street](https://scp-wiki.wikidot.com/scp-9312) before being relocated to Site-19 in 1980. There are no faces in the masks.
