**Subject:** Discovery of the Sanctuary of Isis and Mater Magna  

Dear Conrad,  

I am writing to confirm the discovery of a sanctuary in Mainz, related to our ongoing research. The excavation, conducted in a single day with the utmost discretion, revealed the entrance to the temple on April 9. The structure measures approximately 15 square meters and contains a large number of artifacts yet to be cataloged, associated with the goddesses Mater Magna and Isis, including several potentially anomalous objects.  

I must also inform you that this discovery has drawn the attention of the High Council. A delegation of researchers, reporting directly to the Council, conducted a preliminary study of the site over the past two days. Upon their return from the sanctuary, I was instructed to leave my position at Laboratory CHE:5346 to form and lead a team dedicated exclusively to the study of these artifacts. Due to the specific regulations of West Germany, this team will need to include international members: one American researcher, one Soviet, one French-speaking, and one German.

Accordingly, I am reporting for reassignment to establish Laboratory DEU:6657, affiliated with the C.O.R.A.I.L. network, in accordance with the Council’s directives. An adjacent building to the sanctuary will be adapted to house both the artifacts and the research team.  

I would like to thank you for everything I have learned within your team, particularly your rigor in documentation both, bibliographic and laboratory management, which I intend to apply in my new role.  

Respectfully,  

Johann Froben

Budget and projected timeline in case of mission modification

Laboratory DEU:6657 is required to provide a detailed report on its artifact sorting and study procedures, accompanied by a work plan approved by the Archaeology Department management. *In accordance with Protocol #465/B5 on potentially premonitory dreams of SCP Foundation agents, any agent who believes they have dreamed of a future situation must notify their superior. The superior is required to transcribe the agent’s testimony **in full**, as well as the event if it occurs. The report must be submitted **on the same day** to the Division of Hazy Memories.*

 **DATE OF PREMONITION:**  12/04/1952  

 **DREAMING AGENT:**  Dr. Gerasim Lebedev, researcher  

 **REPORT AUTHOR:**  Dr. Johann Froben, researcher  

 **WITNESS(ES):**  Dr. Katharina Gerlachin, researcher  

 **DESCRIPTION OF THE DREAM AND CONTEXT OF TESTIMONY:**   

Gerasim came to my office this morning at 09:05. He requested a private meeting. I locked the door to ensure the confidentiality of our discussion. He then indicated that he had had a strange dream. With his consent, I began recording our exchange.  

In this dream, he saw himself seated in the same spot as during the testimony. I was present, standing near the door, holding a piece of paper. Katharina was also at the entrance. Gerasim reported that he felt a fear so intense that it still persists while he speaks. A fear of being trapped. His behavior seemed alarming to me: stammering, hesitation, frantic glances around him, heavy sweating, whereas he is usually calm, observant, and quiet.  

He specified that in this dream, Katharina and I shared his fear. An immense, icy fear that ran through his entire body. He added that he knew its cause: "That thing, in the room in front of your office."  

However, I know there is no room in front of my office, just a wall facing outside. This initially reassured me. He added: "I would not want to see the expression on that thing’s face for even one more second." He confided that he wished to confront his dream with reality to free himself. I therefore began the premonition verification protocol.

@@ @@

@@ @@

**DESCRIPTION OF THE EVENT (even if premonitory dream proves false):**

Before we began, someone knocked at the door. I stood to open it. It was Katharina, coming to hand me a list of classified objects. She extended the paper to me. Upon receiving it, I noticed that Gerasim had turned pale. I myself felt a slight shiver.  

The situation corresponded precisely to the description Gerasim had given of his dream. Nevertheless, such a coincidence does not justify classification as a premonition.  

But Gerasim tilted his head, trying to see beyond Katharina, through the partially open door. I then escorted her to the exit, and Gerasim followed me.  

It was at that moment that I nearly jumped: a door stood in the wall where there should have been only a smooth surface, just in front of my office. Composing myself, I asked Katharina if she had seen it before knocking. She answered no, and reminded me that this door appeared in none of the reorganization plans of the Site that she had supervised. She also seemed frightened.  

The three of us found ourselves in front of this door. Gerasim was trembling. I decided not to open it in order to preserve his mental health.  

The door opened on its own, with a violent crash, as if something inside had forced it open. We all jumped. The rest is visible in the attached photograph: a form I would describe as a shrunken head, reminiscent of a stillborn child’s head, atop a mass of shapeless flesh, extended by disproportionately large upper appendages. Most notably, this face seemed to be staring at us.  

Gerasim immediately lost consciousness. We fled, carrying his body to the infirmary, calling Sarah, who has medical training.  

**EXPLANATION OR HYPOTHESIS (if any):**

While Sarah treated Gerasim, diagnosing an anxiety attack, Katharina and I returned to the impossible room. We took all precautions to assess the possibility that an unpredictable and dangerous object had infiltrated the Site’s enclosure.

= Integrated Nomenclature of the Anomalous

2. The Four Original Nomenclatures

To build a robust nomenclature capable of covering the specificities of all domains of the Anomalous, our team devoted the months from October 1952 to January 1953 to developing its own classification system. Each of us studied 75 objects, one-third of which were in fact non-anomalous but were incorporated into the classifications. Here are the four nomenclatures obtained at the end of these four months of research:

Goddard's Socio‑Functional Nomenclature

Based on the social impact level of anomalous objects, their collective uses, and the protocols of contact between humans and these objects during their periods of use.

Social impact

Amplitude (individual, group, institution, mass) and direction (cohesion, schism).

Collective uses

Emerging practices; deviations; normalization of routines around the object.

Contact protocols

Gestures, words, and required constraints; tolerance for deviations; risk of escalation.

Anomalous externalities

Effects on norms, power, symbolic violence; behavioral propagation.

= 6

@@ @@

= Integrated Nomenclature of the Anomalous

Froben's Chrono-Material Nomenclature 

A more anarchaeologically-oriented classification, based on the discovery period, materials used, provenance, and anomalous states of the objects.

Period

Absolute or relative dating; presence of anachronism or non-temporality induced by the object.

Materials / Techniques

Identified or unknown substances; impossible processes; observable self-repair or self-alteration.

Provenance / Context

Place of emergence; mode of appearance (excavation, manifestation, translation); documented initial trajectory.

Anomalous states

Morphological stability or variability; reversibility; non-Newtonian physical behaviors.

Gerlachin's Ritual-Symbolic Nomenclature 

A nomenclature focused on the ritual value of the object.

Ritualization Index

Codification of gestures and roles of the object.

Iconographic Inventory

Standardized recording of motifs associated with the object.

Verbal / Paralinguistic Profile

Measures of oral productions if the object is audible: % of formulaic statements, prosody, rate, lexical stability between sessions.

Contextual Radiation

Spatial range, group size, duration, diffusion coefficient of rituals associated with the object.

= 7

@@ @@

= Integrated Nomenclature of the Anomalous

Lebedev's Econo-Occult Nomenclature 

A nomenclature that studies the occult economy of the circulation and exchange of anomalous objects.

Possession Circuit

Chain of owners; channels (legal, clandestine, spontaneous); transfer inertias/accelerations.

Anomalous Value

Dynamics (growth, collapse, chaotic) and non-economic indexations (events, rituals).

Occult Exchange Networks

Intermediations, hubs, parallel markets; couplings and cross-effects with other anomalies.

Imposed Ownership Regime

Exclusive, collective, non-appropriable, conditional (rite, required consideration).

3. Towards an Integrated Nomenclature, or How to Rethink the Anomalous

The presentation of these four nomenclatures left us with a sense of incompleteness. Although each approach allowed us to isolate relevant characteristics, we knew that a central dimension was missing.

During our research on the various artifacts at our disposal, several elements struck us:

= 8

@@ @@

= Integrated Nomenclature of the Anomalous

- Dr. Goddard described two votive masks sharing the same rite in two distinct rooms. When they were installed simultaneously, twilight chants could be heard in the central corridor. Her Socio‑Functional Nomenclature could not account for such a phenomenon of transversal communion.

- Dr. Froben noted that a normal contemporary object resonated with an anomalous archaic fragment, causing the opening of a timeless micro-fracture as if the two were playing in concert. His Chrono-Material Nomenclature did not anticipate this type of anachronistic potentialization.

- Dr. Gerlachin demonstrated that matching motifs between two artifacts drew a "sacred geometry" in space, inducing a folding of the room proportional to the ritual incantation. The effect did not arise from the object alone, but from their relationship with the environment. Her Ritual-Symbolic Nomenclature could not formalize it.

- Finally, Dr. Lebedev observed a transfer of value between an anomalous statuette and a normal reliquary: the statuette progressively "devaluing" the furniture, which became an offering altar. His Econo-Occult Nomenclature described possession circuits, but not this relational evolution between anomaly and environment.

These phenomena forced us to reconsider our approach. We needed more than a classification tool that merely describes essences ("this object is made of this material", "this artifact belongs to this period"). We required a system that describes interactions.

= 9

@@ @@

= Integrated Nomenclature of the Anomalous

We therefore propose the following definition:

An object is anomalous from the moment that:

- Its actions on the environment do not correspond to the expected effects,

- Its environment does not have the expected effect on it.

In other words: **the anomaly is revealed in the links**.  

Artifacts are not defined solely by their substance, period, symbolism, or value : they are defined above all by what arises around them. An artifact is never isolated: it acts as a node in a network that includes the normal world, the locations that host it, and sometimes even the researchers who observe it.

We now consider that a complete description of an anomalous object must focus on its links with both anomalous and normal environments:  

- proximity links, which contract or expand space and/or time,  

- resonance links, which amplify phenomena when coupled,  

- triggering links, where one activates another,  

- capture links, where one absorbs the function or value of another.

Thus is born the foundation of our Integrated Nomenclature: not a catalog of objects, but a mapping of the relationships that shape their space.

<summary>Additional Information</summary>

The information contained in SCP-8542-I, II, and III files has not been corroborated by investigations conducted after their discovery:

- No temporary Site of Mainz was operational between 1952 and 1953. No building of this type was ever established.  

- No DEU:6657 team is listed in the CORAIL network laboratory records. The researchers mentioned in the file did exist, but their colleagues in their original units lost all trace of them upon their departure. Each assumed they had joined another Foundation structure, but the information chain on these agents was interrupted at that time.

- The general secretariat of the CORAIL network at the time retains a "diffuse memory" of discussions concerning the establishment of a German laboratory, a project that was never realized without explanation.  

- Similarly, there was discussion of opening an archaeological site around a sanctuary of Isis and Mater Magna in Mainz, but no project was validated or launched. The Foundation never located such a sanctuary due to the lack of precise coordinates.

The SCP Foundation nevertheless recovered this file in the ruins of a decommissioned building in Mainz, FRG, following its fire on May 1, 1953.  

Although no official archive confirms the existence of the Site, the team, or the sanctuary, this anomalous document, recounting nonexistent events, was recorded under designation 8542 by the Division of Hazy Memories and is kept as a precaution, and possibly, for containment.
